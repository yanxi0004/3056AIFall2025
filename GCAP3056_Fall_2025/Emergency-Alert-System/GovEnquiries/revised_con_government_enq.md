# Revised Consolidated Government Enquiries - Emergency Alert System Analysis

## Overview

This document consolidates all government enquiries for the Emergency Alert System (EAS) research project. Each section contains enhanced, specificity-focused questions designed to elicit concrete, citable facts that directly support SCMP letter arguments and ARP development.

**Project Details:**
- Course: Taking a Stand: Turning Research Insights into Policy Recommendations
- Project: Analysis of the usage of Emergency Alert System in Hong Kong
- Timeline: 21-day response period for all enquiries
- Purpose: Non-partisan public-interest analysis for university policy project

## 1. CEO Office Enquiries

**Context**: The CEO Office oversees overall government policy and resource allocation. The EAS was launched on November 26, 2020, with a reported development cost of HK$150 million. Public concerns have been raised about limited usage during significant emergencies, such as the September 2023 rainstorm.

### Set 1: Budget Transparency and Cost-Effectiveness

**Questions:**

1. Please provide the EAS's annual operating and maintenance expenditure for each financial year since launch (FY2020–21 to present), broken down by:
   - Staffing (FTEs and grades)
   - Vendor contracts (names, amounts, scope)
   - Hardware/software (licenses, CB platform)
   - Mobile operator reimbursements
   - Testing/exercises
   - Public education/outreach
   - Contingency/renewal funds

2. Please provide a breakdown of the HK$150M development cost by line item (procurement lots, vendors, dates, deliverables). If some items are classified, indicate category, value band, and legal basis for non-disclosure.

3. Provide planned capital refresh/replacement timeline (and budget) for the next 5 years.

**Contextual Information**: The EAS was implemented in 2020 with a development cost of HK$150 million, as reported in various media outlets. Understanding the financial investment will help assess the system's cost-effectiveness.

**Argument Development**: This information will support arguments for "wasted investment" if costs are high with minimal usage, or "lack of transparency" if costs are withheld. Budget data will justify recommendations for optimal resource utilization.

### Set 2: Activation Criteria and Decision-Making Process

**Questions:**

1. Provide the latest written criteria/SOP for categorizing emergencies and activating EAS (document title, version, date). If guidance is embedded in the Emergency Response System (ERS), cite sections and attach extracts.

2. Provide the approval chain (roles/departments), maximum target time from incident detection to broadcast, and thresholds that favor EAS over other channels.

3. Share two anonymized case studies (one activated, one declined) from the past 24 months, including rationale.

**Contextual Information**: Public criticism arose due to the non-utilization of the EAS during significant emergencies like the September 2023 rainstorm. The system is designed for "unforeseen emergency situations that may cause territory-wide/large-scale outage of critical infrastructure or extensively endanger lives and properties."

**Argument Development**: Understanding the decision-making criteria will help identify gaps in current activation protocols and advocate for clearer guidelines. This supports arguments for "underutilization" and "missed opportunities."

### Set 3: Contingency Planning and Infrastructure Resilience

**Questions:**

1. Provide SOPs for major emergencies with telecom or power degradation: which channels precede/follow EAS, and which are non-digital (sirens, PA, radio, TV takeover)?

2. List alternative channels if EAS is unavailable: their reach, activation time, and responsible lead.

3. Provide any tabletop or live exercise reports that assessed these contingencies in the last 24 months.

**Contextual Information**: The EAS relies on cell broadcast service technology, which may be vulnerable during infrastructure failures. The system's effectiveness depends on mobile network infrastructure.

**Argument Development**: This information will support arguments for developing backup communication systems and enhancing infrastructure resilience. Responses will strengthen the "single point of failure" argument for the second letter.

## 2. Security Bureau Enquiries

**Context**: The Security Bureau oversees Hong Kong's Emergency Response System and coordinates emergency responses across various departments. According to the Secretary for Security's statement on October 18, 2023, "under truly justified emergency situations, B/Ds may disseminate EAS Messages through the EAS terminals to enable immediate responsive actions by the public."

### Set 1: Expanded Usage for Public Safety and Criminal Threats

**Questions:**

1. List incident categories currently eligible for EAS (e.g., violent offender at large, explosives, CBRN, child abduction). For each category, provide criteria and example message templates.

2. Has the Bureau considered adopting Amber/Blue/Silver-style alerts? If not, provide reasons and review timeline. If yes, share pilot results or decision memos.

3. Provide examples (last 24 months) where EAS was considered for public safety incidents but not activated, with rationale.

**Contextual Information**: International best practices show that emergency alert systems can be effectively used for various public safety threats beyond extreme emergencies. The current system appears limited to extreme scenarios only.

**Argument Development**: This information will support arguments for expanding EAS usage beyond extreme scenarios to include daily public safety concerns, demonstrating the system's underutilization. Responses will strengthen the "missed opportunities" argument.

### Set 2: Inter-Departmental Coordination and Emergency Response Protocols

**Questions:**

1. Provide the inter-department activation workflow (RACI) for EAS, with specific SLA targets (detection-to-draft, draft-to-approval, approval-to-broadcast).

2. Provide after-action review (AAR) summaries for the largest three emergencies since 2020, noting any EAS-related decisions and lessons learned.

3. Provide coordination mechanisms between Security Bureau and other departments during EAS activation, including decision-making hierarchy and escalation procedures.

**Contextual Information**: The Security Bureau's Emergency Response System outlines policies and operations for various emergencies, but coordination mechanisms during EAS activation remain unclear. Public concerns have been raised about limited activation during significant emergencies.

**Argument Development**: Understanding coordination mechanisms will highlight strengths and weaknesses in the current system, informing recommendations for improved inter-departmental collaboration. This supports arguments for "system optimization" and "better coordination."

### Set 3: Public Communication and System Testing

**Questions:**

1. Provide official guidance for citizens on receiving/responding to alerts (URL, languages, last update). Include behavioral instructions by alert type.

2. Provide testing cadence (past tests, dates, scope, delivery metrics), false-positive/false-negative stats (last 24 months), and outreach metrics (reach, comprehension, satisfaction).

3. Provide public education initiatives since 2020 (campaigns, languages, channels) and KPIs (awareness, opt-out rates if applicable).

**Contextual Information**: Public criticism arose due to the non-utilization of the EAS during significant emergencies, indicating gaps in public communication and feedback mechanisms. The Security Bureau has conducted large-scale exercises and departmental training for emergency services.

**Argument Development**: This information will support arguments for regular system evaluations and comprehensive public education campaigns to maintain reliability and ensure widespread understanding. Responses will strengthen the "public education" argument.

## 3. OFCA Enquiries

**Context**: OFCA is responsible for the technical implementation and maintenance of Hong Kong's Emergency Alert System (EAS), which employs cell broadcast service technology to disseminate messages to mobile users during emergencies. The system was launched on November 26, 2020, and relies on mobile network infrastructure to reach citizens.

### Set 1: Cost Analysis and System Coverage

**Questions:**

1. Provide OFCA's annual spend on EAS platform, testing, and compliance monitoring by year since 2020.

2. Provide device compatibility coverage: percent of active SIMs/devices that are EAS-capable; breakdown by OS/version; share the methodology and last measurement date.

3. Provide the current "supported devices" list URL and update cadence.

**Contextual Information**: The EAS was implemented in 2020 with a development cost of HK$150 million, and only mobile devices that support cell broadcast service technology can receive EAS messages. Device compatibility may be a limiting factor in system effectiveness.

**Argument Development**: Understanding the financial investment and device compatibility will help assess the system's cost-effectiveness and identify gaps in coverage, supporting arguments for broader accessibility initiatives. This supports the "wasted investment" or "coverage gaps" arguments.

### Set 2: Infrastructure Vulnerability and Contingency Planning

**Questions:**

1. Clarify dependency stack: whether cell broadcast requires data connectivity or only signaling; tolerance to power loss, core/backhaul outages; dependencies on undersea cables; roaming behavior for visitors.

2. Provide results of resilience tests (e.g., simulated partial MNO outages, disaster cell on wheels, priority restoration).

3. List alternative channels available if CB fails, with expected reach and time-to-air.

**Contextual Information**: The EAS relies on cell broadcast service technology, which may be vulnerable during infrastructure failures, creating a single point of failure. The system's effectiveness depends entirely on mobile network infrastructure.

**Argument Development**: This information will support arguments for developing backup communication systems and enhancing infrastructure resilience to address critical vulnerabilities. Responses will strengthen the "single point of failure" argument for the second letter.

### Set 3: System Testing and Public Awareness

**Questions:**

1. Provide the last 6 test dates, participating MNOs, message delivery rates, handset receipt rates, and geographic coverage anomalies.

2. Provide outreach initiatives since 2020 (campaigns, languages, channels) and KPIs (awareness, opt-out rates if applicable).

3. Provide technical specifications and capabilities of the EAS system, including limitations and constraints.

**Contextual Information**: OFCA has released press statements and maintains a webpage detailing the EAS and compatible devices, but public awareness appears limited. The system has been operational for nearly four years with minimal public education.

**Argument Development**: This information will support arguments for regular system evaluations and comprehensive public education campaigns to ensure widespread understanding of the EAS. Responses will strengthen the "lack of public education" argument.

### **Expected Response Timeline:**

- **Week 1-2**: Initial responses from most departments
- **Week 3**: Follow-up responses and clarifications

### **Response Quality Indicators:**

- **Comprehensive**: Detailed answers with specific examples, documents, and metrics
- **Partial**: Some questions answered with specific data, others vague or unavailable
- **Limited**: Minimal information provided, mostly general statements
- **Non-responsive**: Questions not addressed or referred to public websites

### **Follow-up Strategy:**

- **Day 10**: Mid-way check-in to ensure responses are on track
- **Day 21**: Final deadline with follow-up for incomplete responses
- **Post-deadline**: Analysis of response quality and integration into research

This revised approach ensures systematic tracking of all enquiries and strategic use of responses to strengthen policy arguments with concrete, citable facts.
